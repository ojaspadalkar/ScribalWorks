Misunderstandings Across Time

An Assyrian Anecdote as Prologue

History often hides ironies in plain sight. Consider the "Assyrian" identity, a name resurrected in the 19th century by outsiders and eagerly adopted by local communities, despite its ancient bearers having vanished long ago. In the 1840s, British explorer Austen Henry Layard excavated the ruins of ancient Assyria along the upper Tigris, encountering local Nestorian and Chaldean Christian groups who spoke varieties of Neo-Aramaic and had practiced their faith in remote mountain fastnesses for centuries. Mistakenly believing these Aramaic-speaking Christians to be the direct biological and cultural descendants of the vanished Assyrian empire, Layard popularized the idea that they were "Assyrians." American and Anglican missionaries soon reinforced this notion, introducing concepts of nationhood and encouraging these communities to identify with Assyria's storied past. They built schools, printed texts, and wrote histories that placed these mountain Christians in a direct line from Ashurbanipal and Sennacherib. By the late 19th century, a nationalist awakening saw these Christians revive the term Āthorāyā (Assyrian) as their self-designation, building a new national pride around the ancient Assyrian empire rather than their traditional church sects.

Over time, even the church itself was renamed. What had been known simply as the Nestorian Church of the East was rebranded in the 1970s as the "Assyrian Church of the East," cementing the new identity in ecclesiastical stone. This modern Assyrian narrative, born from a translation error, missionary re-interpretation, and cultural pride, is a cautionary tale of how easily historical truth can be distorted. A name plucked from antiquity was applied to a people with a different heritage, creating a proud national story that, strictly speaking, rests on a misidentification.

This phenomenon of misrepresented truth is not unique. Throughout history, cultures have reinterpreted and even demonized each other's heritage due to linguistic confusion, political agendas, or theological rivalry. In the chapters that follow, we journey back several millennia to examine one of the most profound examples of such a divergence: the ancient Indo-IranianIndo-IranianThe branch of the Proto-Indo-European family that gave rise to both the Vedic (Sanskrit) and Iranian (Avestan, Old Persian) language groups. Also called Aryan from the self-designation Arya. peoples, forebears of the Vedic Indians and the Avestan Zoroastrian Iranians, who took the same set of ancestral gods and split them into opposing camps of "devas" and "asuras" (or "ahuras").

We will see how deep cultural misunderstandings and subtle shifts in meaning turned one group's gods into the other's demons, and vice versa. Along the way, we will trace their common origin, explore how translation quirks and sectarian pride fed this mutual demonization, and compare it with similar splits in other traditions: from Norse myths to Mesopotamian lore, from the Jewish exile in Babylon to the illuminated manuscripts of medieval Persia. The goal is to unveil how a shared ancient legacy was reframed into a tale of good versus evil, and to understand the philosophical consequences that echo in Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, Buddhism, and beyond.

This is a story of divided gods. It is a historical narrative about how one people's deity became another's devil, and what that reveals about truth and misunderstanding in human tradition. It is also, ultimately, a story about ourselves.

What made the Proto-Indo-Europeans so consequential was not merely their genes but their technology and ideas. This combination of mobile logistics and mounted scouting created the conditions for one of history's most consequential population movements. DNA studies in the 21st century have confirmed what linguists long suspected: a massive Yamnaya-related expansion carried Proto-Indo-European language and culture from the steppe into Europe to the west and into South Asia to the southeast. Deep Dive: The Sintashta Culture and the World's First Chariots

Sintashta-Petrovka (c. 2100-1800 BCE)

The most archaeologically vivid window into the Proto-Indo-Iranian world is the Sintashta culture, located in the southern Ural steppes of modern Russia and Kazakhstan. Sintashta settlements were remarkable: fortified towns of up to 40 enclosed structures arranged in an oval or rectangle around a central street, surrounded by walls of timber-reinforced earthen or stone construction with external ditches. Sintashta, the type site, was roughly 160 meters in diameter and contained perhaps 40 to 50 households. It was not a city in the Mesopotamian sense, but it was organized, defended, and clearly the product of collective labor guided by some form of social authority.

What makes Sintashta world-historically significant is its burial record. In the kurgan cemeteries associated with these settlements, archaeologists have found what David Anthony and others have identified as the world's oldest certain chariots. These were not the solid-wheel carts of Sumerian origin, but true spoke-wheeled war chariots: lightweight vehicles with spoked wheels, designed to be pulled at speed by horses trained in pairs with a specialized bit. The chariots were buried with their horses and drivers, along with bronze weapons including axes, daggers, and socketed spearheads. Some burials include cheekpieces for horse bits made of bone or antler, the first clear evidence of horse-drawn vehicular control designed for battle.

Military Implications

The war chariot was to the Bronze Age what the tank was to the 20th century: a platform that combined mobility, shock, and psychological terror. A chariot driven by a trained pair of horses could achieve speeds of 25-30 miles per hour in short bursts, carrying an archer who could loose arrows with devastating accuracy while remaining largely invulnerable to foot soldiers without specialized weapons. The spread of chariot warfare from the steppe across the Near East and South Asia in the 2nd millennium BCE was rapid and transformative. The Hittites deployed them at Kadesh; the Egyptians adopted them against the Hyksos; and the Indo-Aryans may have used them in their movements into the Indian subcontinent.

The Sintashta people were also prolific metallurgists, mining copper from the southern Ural region and producing bronze arsenical alloys that were technically superior to much contemporary Near Eastern metalwork. Finds include casting molds, slag heaps, and finished weapons in quantities suggesting organized production rather than craft production. In their burial mounds, we find the portrait of a martial aristocracy: men buried with full weapon sets, women with elaborate ornaments, and sacrificed horses and cattle accompanying the dead into the afterlife.

Religious Continuity

The religious practices reflected in Sintashta burials show unmistakable connections to both later Vedic and Zoroastrian traditions. Horse sacrifice (aśvamedha in Sanskrit, yasna in Avestan) is attested archaeologically. The preparation of a ritual drink, likely a precursor to Vedic soma and Avestan haoma, is inferred from botanical finds in some contexts, including traces of ephedra and hemp. Fire altars or ritual hearths appear in some settlements. The dead are buried with careful attention to orientation, often east-west aligned, suggesting cosmological significance to celestial direction. When we read the Rigveda's hymns about the great horse sacrifice or the Gathas' mention of fire ritual, we are hearing echoes that trail back to Sintashta.

The Sacred Vocabulary: Two Words That Divided a World

One striking testament to the shared origin of the Indo-Iranians lies in the very words they used for "god" and "lord." Linguists reconstruct two key terms in Proto-Indo-Iranian (and ultimately Proto-Indo-European) religious vocabulary: daywás (from PIE deywós) and hásuras (from PIE h₂nsuros or ansu-). These two words would eventually become the axis around which an entire theological schism revolved.

The first term, deywós, carried the evocative meaning of "a celestial being," literally a shining one, related to words for the sky and brightness. The root is connected to PIE *dyew-, meaning "sky" or "to shine," and it is cognate to Latin deus, Old Norse Týr, and English divine. In Sanskrit this became deva; in Avestan, daeva. Both words are phonologically identical up to a regular sound shift: the v in Sanskrit corresponds to a v in Avestan, but the d in Sanskrit corresponds to a d in Avestan too, so the divergence was semantic, not phonological. The same word in two sister languages came to mean "god" in one and "demon" in the other.

Crucially, these were not two utterly opposed categories but rather a hierarchy within the divine. The hásuras were understood as a subset of the daywáses: in simple terms, the lords among the gods. All asuras/ahuras were by definition also devas/daevas since they were a class of high gods, but not all devas were asuras. Only the mightiest ones, the chiefs and rulers of cosmos, earned that title of "lord." This layered concept is vividly paralleled in other Indo-European traditions.

The Old Norse speakers, distant cousins of the Indo-Iranians, used tívar (cognate to deva, meaning any god in the generic sense) alongside Æsir (cognate to asura, meaning the powerful lord-gods) for the leading tribe of gods like Odin and Thor. The word Týr (singular of tívar) designated a specific sky-god who had once been the supreme deity before Odin's ascendancy, preserving in his very name and diminished role the ancient hierarchy: the "shining one" (tiva/deva type) had been surpassed by the "powerful lord" (Aesir/asura type). Even the Hittites of Anatolia retained a related idea: their word ḫaššu (from the same root as asura) came to mean "king," implying a ruler vested with divine life-or-death authority. The king was the earthly asura, the human embodiment of divine lordship.

Key Insight

The word "asura" began as praise, not condemnation. The great sky-god Varuṇa, the sun-covenant Mitra, the fire Agni: all are called Asuras in the oldest Vedic hymns, as titles of honor. The same root that became Sanskrit's word for "demon" was, in Iran, elevated to the name of God Himself: Ahura Mazda, "the Wise Lord." This inversion, identical words traveling in opposite moral directions, is perhaps the most dramatic semantic reversal in the history of religion.

The Proto-Indo-European Steppe Homeland and Migration Vectors (c. 3500-1500 BCE)

As archaeologist David Anthony describes in meticulous detail, these migrations spanned the steppes and beyond: one branch moved southeast into the Indian subcontinent forming the Vedic Aryans, while another moved southwest toward Iran and formed the Iranian Aryans. By the mid-2nd millennium BCE, the steppe nomads of the Sintashta-Petrovka culture, likely Proto-Indo-Iranians, were forging weapons and the world's first true chariots. They became the conduit of a shared heritage that would later split into two of antiquity's great spiritual traditions: the Vedic religion of India and the Iranian religion of Zoroaster.

Yet at this dawn of the Indo-Iranian world, those future divisions were unknown. The early Indo-Iranians presumably worshipped a pantheon inherited from their forebears: a sky god, sun and moon, fire, wind, and water deities, and figures analogous to MitraMitra/MithraThe most diplomatically attested of the shared Indo-Iranian gods, appearing in the Mitanni treaties (c. 1380 BCE), the Rigveda, and the Avesta. Sacrifices of fire and soma/haoma were performed to commune with the divine. In this primordial stage, "deva" was simply a god and "asura" (or ahura in Iranian) meant a powerful lord, perhaps a subset of gods revered for their might or wisdom. There was no absolute moral dichotomy inherent in the terms.

As one comparative study notes, in the earliest Vedic texts all supernatural beings, bright or dark, could be called devas or asuras without implying one was inherently good or evil. It was a time of ambiguous divinity, when gods could be both beneficent and wrathful, and cosmic dualism had not yet cleaved the pantheon in two.

Chapter Two

Twin Children of the Proto-Indo-Europeans

The Indo-Iranian Brotherhood, Shared Myths and a World Almost Never Divided

As centuries passed, the Indo-Iranian tribes settled into their new homelands: some in the fertile river plains of India, others amid the highlands, oases, and arid plateaus of Iran and Central Asia. They remained cognizant of being one people, calling themselves AryaArya (Sanskrit) / Airya (Avestan)"Noble" or "of good family." The shared self-designation of both the Vedic Indo-Aryans and the ancient Iranians. The word was an ethnocultural marker, not a racial one, though later European racial science catastrophically distorted its meaning., "noble," even as dialects and customs slowly diverged. Their religions at this stage were essentially one tradition in different locales.

We can imagine wandering priests and bards who, had they met, would recognize each other's hymns. Both realms revered the supreme order of ṛta/aša: the Rigveda praises ṛta as the truth that sustains sun and moon, while the Avesta upholds aša as the divine law of righteousness. Both traditions personified this order in gods: Vedic Varuṇa is called "King of Asura, upholder of ṛta," while Avestan Ahura Mazda is described as "maintainer of aša." The two religions are singing the same song in slightly different keys.

Crucially, early Indo-Iranian religion did not pit its gods against each other as inherently good or evil. On the contrary, they preserved a hierarchical but fluid view. The term asura/ahura seems to have been a title of honor, "lord," applied to certain great gods among the devas/daevas ("gods" generally). To put it simply, all asuras were also devas (gods), but not all gods qualified as exalted lords. The kingly Varuṇa, for instance, is called an Asura who rules over both gods and mortals in Rigveda 2.27.10. Likewise Mitra and Varuṇa together are lauded as "Asuras of the devas," lords among the gods. This usage has striking parallels in other Indo-European traditions: in Old Norse, the generic word for gods tívar (cognate to deva) coexisted with a special term Æsir (cognate to asura) for the chief gods like Odin and Thor, the high rulers of Asgard. In Hittite, similarly, the cognate ḫaššu came to mean a supreme ruler or king, again reflecting that an "asura-type" term denoted elevated authority.

All this suggests that the Indo-Iranians originally saw devas and asuras not as opposing camps but as overlapping categories: the asuras/ahuras were the senior gods, embodiments of regal power and guardian wisdom, while the devas/daevas were gods at large, shining ones upholding the cosmos. No hint of demons or moral dualism colored these words at the outset.

Deep Dive: The Mitanni Kingdom — Indo-Iranian Gods in a Mesopotamian Treaty

The Mitanni Connection (c. 1400 BCE)

One of the most startling pieces of evidence for the pre-schism unity of the Indo-Iranians comes not from India or Iran at all, but from Syria. Around 1380 BCE, the Mitanni kingdom of northern Mesopotamia (centered in modern northern Syria and southeastern Turkey) negotiated a peace treaty with the Hittite empire. The treaty was sealed with oaths invoking divine witnesses, and among the gods called upon to guarantee the treaty are names unmistakably from the Indo-Iranian tradition: Mi-it-ra (Mitra), U-ru-wa-na (Varuṇa), In-da-ra (Indra), and Na-sa-at-ti-ia (Nasatya). The Mitanni royal family bore names like Artatama, Artashumara, Tushratta, and Biriwaza, all containing the Indo-Iranian root arta- (cognate to Sanskrit ṛta and Avestan aša).

The Mitanni were not themselves fully Indo-Aryan: they ruled over a largely Hurrian-speaking population and were eventually assimilated. But the ruling elite spoke an Indo-Aryan language and observed Indo-Aryan religious forms. A Mitanni horse-training manual from this period, the Kikkuli text written in Cuneiform, uses technical Indo-Aryan terms for counting the laps in horse-training circuits: aika (one), tera (three), panza (five), satta (seven), na (nine). These are unmistakably Sanskrit cognates.

What the Mitanni Tell Us

The Mitanni evidence is invaluable because it is independent of both the Vedic and Avestan traditions. It gives us a snapshot of what Indo-Iranian religion looked like before it diverged fully into the Vedic and Zoroastrian streams, preserved in amber in a foreign diplomatic context. In this snapshot, Mitra, Varuṇa, Indra, and the Nasatyas are all present as honored gods, none of them demons. The treaty was probably made in the first half of the 14th century BCE, by which point the Rigveda's composition was underway but before Zarathustra's reform. The Mitanni are literally witnessing the world before the schism crystallized.

Furthermore, the presence of arta- (cosmic order) in Mitanni royal names shows that the concept of Ṛta/Aša was already fundamental to Indo-Iranian royal ideology at this early date. The kings were named "Righteous" or "He who has the appearance of Arta," indicating that the cosmic order principle was not just a priestly abstraction but a royal legitimating ideology. This would become even more important as the Achaemenid Persians later claimed divine approval from Ahura Mazda specifically as guardians of Asha (truth/order) against the Lie.

Shared Ritual: Fire, Soma, and the Axis of Heaven

The most viscerally shared aspect of Indo-Iranian religion was its ritual life. Both traditions centered on two rites that have left deep marks in the archaeological and textual record: the fire sacrifice and the preparation and consumption of the sacred plant-drink.

The fire altar (yajna in Sanskrit, yasna in Avestan) was the axis around which the Indo-Iranian world turned. Fire was not merely a convenient medium for cooking offerings; it was the divine presence itself, the intermediary between the human and divine realms. When a priest kindled the ritual fire and fed it oblations of clarified butter (ghee), grain, and the sacred drink, he was not merely performing a ceremony but literally feeding the gods, sustaining the cosmic order. The Vedic fire god Agni himself, as priest and messenger, carried the offerings upward in his smoke to the divine realm. In the Zoroastrian tradition, the sacred fire (Atar) burning in a fire temple represents Ahura Mazda's divine light made present in the world, and its perpetual maintenance was a religious obligation of the highest order.

Archaeological evidence from the BMAC (Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex), the cultural zone in modern Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and northern Afghanistan that flourished from about 2400 to 1700 BCE, shows large fire installations and altars consistent with the fire-sacrifice tradition. Some BMAC sites have yielded botanical remains including Ephedra and Cannabis, consistent with the preparation of a psychoactive plant-drink in the context of large communal ceremonies. The BMAC was the likely interaction zone where Proto-Indo-Iranian religious practice took a more defined shape before the two streams diverged. It is also possible that the Indo-Iranians absorbed elements from BMAC religion, including particular plant-substances and architectural forms for fire temples.

Within this shared framework, the morality of the gods was complex. The same deity could bless or punish. Varuṇa, solemn lord of law, could be compassionate to devotees but also inflict diseases on oath-breakers. Indra, the storm-god, was celebrated for slaying the dragon Vṛtra and releasing the waters, a heroic act of cosmic order, yet he was also known for his ferocious appetite for soma and occasional impulsive deeds. Such ambiguity was accepted. As the Bhagavad Gītā later philosophizes, all beings have both daiva (divine) and asura (demonic) qualities within them, a reflection of an ancient recognition that good and evil are intertwined in the self. Thus, early on, an "Asura" was not a fiend but a potential within the divine order.

The BMAC Intersection

The Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC), flourishing c. 2400-1700 BCE in modern Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan, has been called the "interaction zone" between Indo-Iranian migrants from the steppe and established agricultural civilizations. Fortified rectangular settlements like Gonur Depe in modern Turkmenistan show massive fire-altar complexes consistent with Indo-Iranian sacred fire practice. BMAC ceramic tradition, with distinctive geometric painted decoration and distinctive compartmented seals, shows elements later replicated in both Vedic and Zoroastrian artistic traditions. Michael Witzel has argued that the BMAC represents an early stage of the convergence that produced both Vedic and Avestan religions.

Chapter Three

The Vedic Path

Devas and Asuras in Early Indian Tradition

In the lands of the Indus and the Gangetic plain, the Indo-Aryans' religion blossomed into what we call the Vedic tradition, flourishing from approximately 1500 to 500 BCE. They composed hymns (śruti, "that which is heard") in praise of a pantheon of Devas: sky gods, storm gods, solar deities, dawn maidens, hearth fire, and sacred soma. These hymns were not written down for centuries; they were memorized in their thousands of verses with extraordinary precision, transmitted from generation to generation through an elaborate mnemonic training that required young Brahmin students to repeat each word forward and backward, in every possible combination, to ensure not a syllable was altered. The Vedic oral tradition is perhaps the greatest feat of human memory in history, and it preserved not just words but an entire world of Indo-Iranian cosmological thought.

In these earliest texts, deva simply meant god, and asura still bore its old honorific sense of mighty lord. Varuṇa, as we saw, is extolled as an Asura, as are Mitra, Agni, and other high gods in various hymns. The Vedic asura was not a fiend but a beneficent patriarch. Varuṇa, for instance, was revered as the upholder of ṛta (cosmic order and truth), wielding a just rod of punishment for oath-breakers and sinners. He surveys all the world from his palace above the heavens, his spies (the stars, perhaps) watching every action of every person, and his cosmic noose (pāśa) ensnares those who violate the truth. He is awesome, austere, and just; a god before whom even the pious tremble and beg forgiveness for unwitting transgressions. In this portrayal, Varuṇa is every inch an Asura in the most exalted sense: a great lord whose authority is total and whose knowledge is complete.

"O Varuṇa, whatever law of thine we have violated as men, have mercy, Asura, have mercy." — Rigveda, hymn addressed to Varuṇa, one of many in which the great god is invoked by his Asura title in the most reverential of contexts.

Rigveda — The Ancient Vedic Hymns, c. 1500-1200 BCE

Yet even as these hymns were being compiled, subtle shifts were underway. Later portions of the Vedic literature, the Brāhmaṇas (liturgical prose texts) and beyond, transitioning into the epic and Purāṇic era, show asura taking on a more negative coloration. How did this happen? One theory suggests a semantic misunderstanding: ancient Brahmin commentators, puzzling over archaic verses, might have mis-segmented the word asura as a-sura, interpreting it as "non-sura," literally "not a god." In Sanskrit, as in Avestan, the prefix a- is a negator (like "un-" or "non-"). Thus, an innocent initial a in asura was mistaken for a prefix, leading those scholars to declare that an "asura" must be something opposed to a sura (deva). The brilliant simplicity of this mistake is matched only by its historical consequences.

This clever but flawed etymology gained traction. Priestly authors started to use sura as a new word for "god" to contrast with asura. In later myths and commentaries, asuras were gradually cast as the anti-gods, cruel, power-hungry beings in perpetual conflict with the Devas. Importantly, this was not a sudden satanization of any beloved deity. No formerly revered Vedic god was outright condemned. Revered figures like Varuṇa or Mitra quietly shed the title "Asura" and were simply counted among the Devas thereafter. The new Asuras that populate post-Vedic myth, including Hiraṇyakaśipu, the father of Prahlāda, the demon king Rāvaṇa of the Rāmāyaṇa, the ocean-churning rivals of the gods, are often drawn not from earlier worshipped gods but from the ranks of exotic or purely mythic beings. In other words, the term Asura was reassigned: it came to denote a class of formidable but malevolent beings, often synonymous with Daitya (descendants of the primordial mother Diti) or Dānava (offspring of Danu) in the mythology.

Other folk etymologies also circulated. Some Purāṇas explain that the Asuras got their name because they rejected the sacred drink sura (wine or the elixir of immortality) during the churning of the ocean, thus "a-sura" meaning "those without sura." Another explanation was that early Sanskrit grammarians, noticing the word sura ("bright, alcoholic drink") and asura, invented the false etymology that asura meant "non-sura (non-god)." Indeed, one theory holds that learned Brahmins consciously stopped calling holy gods "Asura," using "Sura" or Deva instead for positive beings, and relegated Asura to describe only hostile, anti-god forces. This scholastic tweak, if it occurred, was not born of any direct conflict with Iranians: it was an internal linguistic logic that snowballed into a theological change.

The Great Moral Complexity of the Vedic World

By the end of this evolution (around the late Vedic or early epic period), Indian texts clearly distinguish the surās (gods) from the asurās (demons). The Mahābhārata and Purāṇas narrate cosmic battles between Devas and Asuras, analogous to the Greek Olympians versus Titans. And yet, these stories often carry an undertone that the dichotomy is not absolute. Asuras, though antagonists, are still part of the divine play. Even in outright war, Indian myth sometimes blurs the moral lines. Asuras can display virtues like courage, scholarship, and even devotion (Bhakti) to other deities, while Devas are not free from vice. The king of gods Indra is notorious for pride and occasional folly, sometimes committing acts in the epics for which he must suffer consequences. This non-dual acknowledgment of shades of gray is perhaps a direct result of a worldview that did not externalize all evil into a separate set of beings. Yes, there were demons and anti-gods in stories, but philosophically, Hinduism often brought the discussion back to overcoming the demonic within: greed, anger, pride. The Upanishadic tale of Indra and the Asura Virochana jointly seeking the knowledge of the Self from Prajāpati is a brilliant allegory. Both the god and the demon initially lack understanding; the god persists, asks questions, and refines his knowledge (a positive quality), whereas the demon takes the first answer at face value and leaves, using it arrogantly, thus failing to attain truth. The moral is subtle: being a "Deva" or "Asura" is an attitude, not an intrinsic identity. Hinduism thereby kept the door of redemption open. Even Asuras could become wise or devote themselves to God. Mahābali, an Asura king, is promised liberation by Vishnu; the demon king Rāvaṇa, though villainous, is also seen as a great devotee of Lord Śiva in some traditions.

Buddhism, emerging in India around the 5th century BCE, interestingly took a stance somewhat akin to Zoroastrianism in one aspect: it systematized the cosmos into distinct realms of rebirth, including a Deva realm (gods) and an Asura realm (jealous, warlike demigods). In Buddhist cosmology, Asuras are explicitly characterized by envy and belligerence, perpetually fighting the Devas out of resentment. Although Buddhism does not assign cosmic moral evil to the Asuras (they are just another class of unenlightened beings), it does label them as "titans" consumed by negative emotions. In some Buddhist texts, Asuras are even called pūrvadeva ("former gods"), implying they fell from grace, a notion that resonates with the idea that Asuras were once on par with gods, indeed a faint memory of the ancient meaning. But Buddhist tradition, true to its middle path, does not eternalize the dualism: an Asura can be reborn as a human or even a deva once their karma changes, and vice versa.

The Avestan Revolution

Zarathustra's Reform and the Making of Zoroastrianism

Meanwhile, on the Iranian plateau and in Central Asia, the Iranic branch of the Indo-Iranians was experiencing its own religious evolution. By roughly 1200 to 1000 BCE (dates are vigorously debated, with some scholars placing Zarathustra as early as 1500 BCE and others as late as 600 BCE), a spiritual reformer arose among them known to history as ZarathustraZarathustra / ZoroasterThe prophet of the ancient Iranian religion, whose hymns (the Gathas) form the oldest core of the Avesta. He preached a bold new theology that would become Zoroastrianism, one of the world's oldest prophetic religions and arguably the world's first to articulate ethical dualism as its central cosmic principle.

To understand Zarathustra's impact, we must first appreciate the religious milieu he inherited. The pre-Zoroastrian Iranians worshipped a pantheon much like the Vedic one: they venerated daevas (gods) and ahuras (lords) just as their ancestors did. For a time, it seems Ahura was simply a title of honor. The Iranian god Mithra (cognate to Vedic Mitra) could be called an ahura in early usage, and there were likely other figures similar to Varuṇa upholding covenant and order. But at some point before or during Zarathustra's life, a decisive shift occurred: the term Daeva (daēuua in Avestan) became associated with false gods or demons.

The Avesta (Zoroastrian scriptures) portrays the Daevas as malevolent beings who "through their blindness chose the Worst Mind" and turned away from righteousness. They are depicted as worthy of rejection and scorn, followers of the Lie (druj). On the other hand, Ahura was reserved for the highest divinities of the Zoroastrian faith, foremost Ahura MazdaAhura Mazda"The Wise Lord." Alongside him stood the six Amesha Spentas (Holy Immortals): Vohu Manah (Good Mind), Aša Vahišta (Best Truth), Xšaθra Vairya (Desirable Dominion), Spənta Ārmaiti (Holy Devotion), Haurvatāt (Wholeness), and Amərətāt (Immortality). Zarathustra's Hymns: The Gathas as Sacred Poetry

The oldest Zoroastrian texts are the Gathas, seventeen hymns attributed directly to Zarathustra himself. Written in a dialect of Avestan so archaic that it is almost contemporaneous in form with the oldest Rigvedic Sanskrit, the Gathas are remarkable literary documents. They are not narrative myth but visionary poetry of great personal intensity: a prophet wrestling with his god, calling for divine guidance, lamenting the violence of his world, and describing his vision of a cosmic order based on truth versus the lie. In the Gathas, Zarathustra speaks directly to Ahura Mazda in the second person with an intimacy that has no obvious parallel in the Rigveda.

What strikes the reader of the Gathas is how Zarathustra frames the fundamental moral choice facing every conscious being. In his cosmology, at the beginning of existence, two primal Spirits, one holy and one destructive, made a choice. The holy spirit (Spənta Mainyu) chose truth, life, and goodness; the hostile spirit (Angra Mainyu) chose the Lie and destruction. This primordial bifurcation mirrors what happened linguistically to the deva/asura terminology: one path led to the divine, the other to the demonic. And crucially, humans are called to make the same choice in their own lives with their own thoughts, words, and deeds.

Zoroaster never names a single Daeva explicitly in the Gathas. He speaks of them collectively with contempt, but we do not find statements like "Indra is a demon" in the oldest hymns. This suggests that when Zarathustra began preaching, the term daeva was already heading toward a negative connotation, or at least was controversial. His reform might have accelerated an existing trend: perhaps some Iranian communities had started preferring the worship of Ahuras (like Mitra and Varuṇa-equivalents) over other daeva-gods, and seeing those other gods as morally suspect. Under this scenario, Zarathustra gave the final theological justification, proclaiming that those daevas really were demons, delighting in chaos and violence, and that henceforth true religion must reject them entirely.

Deep Dive: The Vendidad — Indra and the Nasatyas as Listed Demons

The Vidēvdāt (Vendidad) — The Law Against Demons

The Vendidad (a corruption of Vidēvdāt, "the law given against the demons") is one of the most important but also most polemical texts in the Zoroastrian Avesta. Written considerably later than the Gathas in form and content, it represents a codification of Zoroastrian ritual law, purity rules, and demonology. It is in the Vendidad that we find the most explicit naming of demonized deities who were honored by the Indo-Aryans.

Vendidad 10.9 lists the chief demons of the Zoroastrian pantheon. Among them, with unmistakable transparency, are:

Indra (Avestan: Indar or Indra) — Yes, the great champion of the Rigveda, the Soma-drinking storm-god and dragon-slayer of the Vedic tradition, appears here as a chief Daeva. In Zoroastrian tradition, Indra is the demon of apostasy, one who leads the faithful away from the righteous path. The theological reasoning is clear: Indra represents the violent, intoxicated warrior archetype that Zarathustra found morally repugnant. In his reform, which emphasized peaceful agriculture, honest craft, and truth-telling over raiding and feasting, a god who embodied the drunken warrior's ethos was anathema. The Gathas themselves condemn the cattle-raiding culture associated with warriors (kavis and karapans), and Indra with his soma-drunk battlefield valor was the perfect symbol of everything Zarathustra opposed.

Saurva (= Sanskrit Śarva, an epithet of Rudra) — Śarva is one of the eight names of Rudra in the Śatarudriya hymn of the Yajurveda. His Avestan counterpart Saurva is cast as a demon of misrule, representing arbitrary violence and chaos. This demonization reflects a pattern: the more violent and unpredictable aspects of the Indo-Iranian pantheon were singled out by Zoroaster.

Nāñhaithya (= Sanskrit Nāsatya, one of the Aśvin twins) — The Aśvins (Nasatyas) were among the most beloved healing deities of the Vedic tradition, divine physicians who rescue humans from disaster and disease. In the Mitanni treaty they appear as Nasatya. In Zoroastrian demonology, their counterpart Nāñhaithya is a demon of arrogance and discontent. The demonization of the healer twins is particularly striking and may reflect specific features of the Aśvin cult (including associations with horse sacrifice or particular ecstatic rituals) that Zoroaster found incompatible with his ethical reform.

Why These and Not Others?

A crucial insight emerges from this list: the Zoroastrian reform did NOT demonize all Indo-Iranian deities. Mithra survived and flourished as an Ahura. Anāhitā (the Iranian Sarasvatī) became one of the most popular Yazatas (beings worthy of worship). Verethragna (the Victory god) absorbed Indra's positive functions. The selective nature of the demonization tells us something important about Zarathustra's reform: he was not attacking all of the old religion but specifically targeting those deities associated with the warrior aristocracy's excesses (cattle-raiding, intoxication, gratuitous violence) that he saw as destructive to the pastoral, agricultural community he championed.

Chapter Five

The Great Schism

Deva and Asura Inversion — The Linguistic and Theological Rupture

At some point in the late Bronze Age, the unity of the Indo-Iranian religious worldview began to crack. As Indo-Aryans and Iranians developed separately, subtle linguistic shifts and perhaps sectarian innovations gave rise to the Great Schism, a symbolic inversion wherein each branch redefined the old labels in mirror-opposite ways. The result was unprecedented in world religion: two closely related faiths effectively swapped the moral polarity of the same divine terminology. In Vedic India, deva remained the word for a god (generally positive), while asura gradually morphed into a term for a malicious being. In Iran, ahura (from asura) was upheld as the noble term for the highest gods, whereas daeva (deva) came to mean a false god, a demon. How did this strange flip occur?

Ancient evidence suggests it was a drawn-out process, not a single event. The Rigveda, our earliest Sanskrit source (c. 1500 to 1200 BCE), still uses asura in a positive sense frequently, calling Varuṇa and Mitra "wise Asuras." But it also contains passages where we see a nascent distinction: one hymn speaks of "Asuras who are Devas" versus "Asuras who are not Devas." This hints that Vedic priests were wrestling with categories: perhaps using asura to denote certain venerable gods, while labeling other spirits or anti-gods as "asura adeva" (asuras that are non-gods). Over time, especially in later Vedic and epic literature, Asura became a byword for the power-hungry, prideful, and morally corrupt enemies of the gods.

The Mirror Paradox

Here is the extraordinary situation that emerged: both the Vedic Indians and the Zoroastrian Iranians continued to worship many of the same gods (Mitra/Mithra, fire, the sacred waters, the forces of cosmic order). But each tradition labeled the other's supreme divine category as its supreme evil category. Vedic Indians heard "ahura" and thought "demon" (for asura had become their word for demon). Zoroastrians heard "deva" and thought "demon" (for daeva had become their word for false god). Two peoples worshipping essentially overlapping values, demonizing each other's theological vocabulary, while neither actually worshipped what the other considered truly evil.

Iranian lore remembered that the Indian cousins had begun speaking of asuras negatively, and they misunderstood this as Indians claiming Ahuras (the Iranians' highest gods) were evil. Feeling their faith under attack, the early Iranians doubled down: they drew a sharper line between their beloved Ahuras and despised Daevas. If Hindu priests said "asuras are demonic," the Iranians would say, "Fine — then Daevas (your devas) are demonic!" So suggests one theory of mutual antagonism: the two groups, originally sharing the terms, each thought the other was slandering their gods, and so retaliated in kind.

Another theory, equally plausible, is that the changes occurred independently: perhaps Zoroaster's reforms came first, and later some Hindu theologians, offended by hearing that daevas (gods like Indra) were called demons by Iranians, decided to avoid asura and thus inadvertently confirmed the Iranian suspicions. Or vice versa: an internal Hindu linguistic shift came first, turning asura into a negative term (without targeting any Iranian god directly), and Iranians, upon hearing that, reacted by codifying their own dichotomy where Daeva became the bad word. In either case, it is clear that by the time of the earliest texts (the Rigveda and the Avesta), both peoples had "deviated from the typical Proto-Indo-Iranian usage, each in their own way." They ended up effectively worshipping each other's demons, at least in name, a curious twist, since in practice their ethical values and even many gods still largely coincided.

As one commentator aptly notes, looking at a handful of divine names in isolation makes Zoroastrianism and Hinduism appear antagonistic, "but with additional context their relationship becomes more clear." Both traditions still honored courage, truth, generosity, and piety; neither actually venerated what the other considered purely evil forces. The divergence was as much semantic and perspectival as it was theological.

Deep Dive: The a-sura Misreading — A Scholarly Error That Changed History

The False Etymology That Became Theology

Sanskrit is an extraordinarily precise language, but even its greatest grammarians were capable of misreading archaic texts. The word asura in its earliest Vedic usage is opaque. Its etymology connects it to Proto-Indo-Iranian *hásura-, meaning "lord" or "powerful life-giver," from a root related to "being" or "vital force." But to a later Brahmin commentator working with a text several centuries old, the word might have looked like a compound of a- (the Sanskrit negative prefix, cognate to Greek an- and English un-) plus sura (a word meaning "god" or "bright being" derived from svar, "sun/brightness").

This reading, a-sura = "non-god" or "non-sura," is phonologically plausible to someone encountering the word without historical context. And once that etymology was accepted in a prestigious commentary or grammatical tradition, it would ripple through subsequent interpretation. If asura means "non-god," then every Rigvedic verse that calls Varuṇa an Asura is now a puzzle: either the verse is calling Varuṇa a non-god (impossible for a revered deity), or it must be interpreted differently. The solution found by later interpreters was sometimes to argue that the original Varuṇa was simply being called "exalted" in a different sense, or to quietly drop the asura designation from later retellings of his stories.

The Parallel False Etymology in Iranian

Interestingly, a parallel creative etymology may have operated in Iranian. The Avestan word daēva was ultimately from the same root as Sanskrit deva. But in the Zoroastrian theological tradition, once daevas were fully demonized, a need arose to explain why they had chosen evil. The answer given in some texts is that the Daevas chose the "Worst Mind" (Aka Manah, opposite of Vohu Manah, Good Mind). The implication is a kind of theological retroactive condemnation: they are evil because they freely chose evil at the beginning of time. This is a cosmogonic rationale, not an etymological one, but it serves the same purpose: explaining away what had once been a neutral or positive term by reframing its bearers as moral failures from the start.

The Consequence: Two Traditions Locked in Inverted Self-Definition

The end result of these linguistic and theological developments was that each tradition came to define its own divine category partly in opposition to the other's. To be Vedic meant to follow the Devas; to be Zoroastrian meant to reject the Daevas. To be Vedic meant to honor the Asuras who were the greatest gods; to be Zoroastrian meant to follow the Ahuras and reject the Daevas. Since Asura and Ahura are the same word, and since Deva and Daeva are the same word, the two traditions were essentially defining themselves as opposite to each other using shared vocabulary. This is the Indo-Iranian schism in its most concentrated form: a linguistic accident amplified by theology into a civilizational divide.

By roughly the mid-1st millennium BCE, the split was complete. In India, the Devas were gods of light and virtue (though not without foibles), while the Asuras became a category of demons, proud titans fated to be vanquished. Later Hindu mythology richly developed the Deva-Asura wars, but often in a morally complex way: Asuras were not devils per se, but beings driven by ambition, frequently portrayed as cousins of the gods rather than ontologically evil. Some texts like the Upanishads even used Deva and Asura symbolically for tendencies within the human psyche, questioning vs. presumptuous attitudes.

Meanwhile in Iran, especially under the influence of Zarathustra's followers and later Zoroastrian theologians, the dichotomy hardened. The Ahuras (and a new term yazata, "worthy of worship") were exclusively the good divinities, led by Ahura Mazda, and the Daevas were denounced as a fixed category of irredeemably evil entities. No Iranian would call a holy being daeva anymore. So deep was this revulsion that by the Achaemenid era (5th century BCE), King Xerxes could proclaim in his famous daiva inscription: "There was a place where previously the daivas were worshipped. By the favor of Ahura Mazda, I destroyed that sanctuary of the daivas, and I forbade the worship of the daivas." This historical record in Old Persian shows the word daiva had come to mean "idol" or false god, a remarkable turnaround for a word rooted in the common Indo-European term for the divine.

Thus the great schism set the two sister civilizations on divergent theological paths. To an outside observer, it might seem as if the Indo-Aryans and Iranians were polarized opposites: one man's god was literally the other man's demon. Yet, as we shall see, appearances deceive. The moral content of their religions did not invert so neatly.

Reflections in Rival Pantheons

Mirror Images, Inversions, and the Gods That Traveled Both Ways

In the wake of the schism, the Vedic and the Zoroastrian traditions evolved elaborate mythologies that often appear as distorted mirror images of one another. Many individual figures and concepts were directly shared but only their narrative framing and valorization differed. By comparing these side by side, we gain insight into how cultural environment and ideology can reshape the same inherited symbols beyond recognition, and yet how those symbols continue to carry the same fundamental human concerns about power, order, truth, and the cosmos.

Take the Storm God IndraIndraThe king of the Vedic Devas, storm-god, soma-drinker, and dragon-slayer. He defeats Vṛtra the cosmic serpent to release the waters of creation. In Avestan tradition he is Indar, a chief Daeva, demon of apostasy. This inversion is perhaps the most vivid illustration of the Indo-Iranian schism.. In the Rigveda, Indra is the colossal champion of the Devas, lauded for smashing the serpent Vṛtra and letting the life-giving rivers flow. He is maghavan (bountiful), the warrior who secures prosperity for his followers. With a body large enough to be fed a thousand vats of soma, he embodies not just military prowess but the intoxicating vitality of life itself. Rain, which can be violent in India's monsoon-ravaged plains, is his gift. Thunder and lightning announce his presence. He is the god of the sky in its most energetic, dynamic, and dangerous mode.

Now peer at the Iranian side. In the Avesta's Vendidad, Indra is named as a chief Daeva, explicitly an evil being opposed to truth. What could turn a celebrated hero into a devil? Part of the answer may lie in geography and climate. The Iranian plateau is largely arid; water is precious and rains are scarce blessings. A god of storms like Indra would be naturally experienced as benevolent in water-scarce Iran, one might think. But the key is not rain as such: it is the culture of Indra worship that Zarathustra found destructive. Indra was the patron of the warrior culture, of cattle-raiding, of soma-drunk feasting after battle. Zarathustra's reform championed the sedentary, agricultural, cattle-herding community against the raiding warrior aristocracy. Indra embodied precisely the values he opposed.

Varuṇa's Journey into Ahura Mazda

Now consider Varuṇa, once the Asura Sovereign of the cosmos. In the Rigveda he is a majestic, if fearsome, upholder of ṛta, who punishes sin with his noose of fate yet also grants insight to sages. By later Hindu times, Varuṇa recedes to become god of the ocean, respected but no longer central to lived religion. The great cosmic lawyer who once governed the stars from his heavenly throne becomes a somewhat demoted figure in the sprawling Hindu pantheon, overshadowed by Vishnu, Shiva, and the great Goddess.

In Iran, however, the spirit of Varuṇa arguably rose to the very top. Ahura Mazda, the supreme Lord, bears striking resemblance to Varuṇa in several aspects. Both are wise, all-seeing, concerned with covenant and cosmic law. Both enforce oaths: Varuṇa binds oath-breakers with his noose; Ahura Mazda judges the souls of the dead at the Chinvat Bridge. Both govern the waters and the sky. Both are called "the wise one" in their respective traditions. The Avesta never names Varuṇa directly (the name is absent from the corpus), but Zoroastrian tradition has Haurvatāt (Wholeness) and Apąm Napāt ("grandson of waters") alongside Mithra, reflecting the watery, law-giving domain of Varuṇa now vested in the Ahuric side.

Some mid-20th century scholars suggested that Zoroastrianism especially exalted a Varuṇa-like figure because in the harsh Iranian climate water and the ethical order were paramount. While simplistic in isolation, the observation holds a kernel of truth: Iranian religion made Ahura Mazda into a composite of the highest divinity traits, including those of an omniscient sky-father and a ruler of cosmic waters. In one Avestan hymn, Mazda is lauded as the god "with good supernatural power, zealous, accompanied by aša (truth)," language that echoes Vedic praise of Mitra and Varuṇa. It is as if the mantle of Varuṇa the Asura King found a new home in the figure of the Wise Lord. Meanwhile, Varuṇa's "exiled" brothers, envisioned in later Indian legend as demons, might correspond to the daevas banished in Zoroastrian lore.

Mitra/Mithra — The One Who Survived Both Ways

Another striking case is Mithra/Mitra. This deity of contracts and oaths was revered on both sides: Mitra in India is often twinned with Varuṇa as friend of mankind, guardian of truth. Mithra in Iran likewise remained immensely popular despite Zarathustra's silence on him, and was classed among the few who could still be called Ahura. Notably, Mithra is one of the very few whom the Avesta occasionally still calls "Ahura" alongside Mazda. In the Hymn to Mithra (Mihir Yasht), he is described as "the lord of wide pastures," "he of the thousand ears," with a hundred-columned palace on the highest summit of Mount Harā.

In India, Mitra is a deva (one of the Ādityas, solar gods). In Iran, Mithra is on the good side as well, worshipped as the god of covenant and sunlight who battles falsehood. The two traditions agree: Mithra/Mitra is beneficent. However, intriguingly, "Mitra" in Sanskrit never attained the absolute primacy that Mithra did in later Iran, where Mithraism even spread westward to become a major mystery religion in the Roman Empire. Roman soldiers from Britain to Mesopotamia worshipped in underground sanctuaries (Mithraea) a version of this Indo-Iranian deity. The image of Mithra slaying a bull in Roman Mithraic iconography likely encodes astronomical symbolism, but the deity's roots are unmistakably in the shared Indo-Iranian tradition. The fact that it was the Iranian version (Mithra as Ahura, as cosmic figure of Truth and the Sun) rather than the Vedic version (Mitra as one of many Ādityas) that conquered the Roman legions is perhaps an index of Zoroastrianism's more concentrated theological investment in this deity.

Deep Dive: Verethragna — Indra's Ghost in Iranian Religion

The Problem with Killing a God

When Zarathustra demonized Indra, he faced a theological problem: Indra's most beloved function — the slaying of obstacles and the granting of victory — was not only morally neutral but actively desirable. The Vedic Indra smote the dragon Vṛtra (whose name means "obstacle" or "coverer") and released the cosmic waters, a mythological encoding of storm-rain fertilizing the earth. Victorious military force and the breaking of cosmic blockages are legitimate needs for any religious tradition.

Zoroastrianism's solution was elegant: it transferred Indra's positive functions to a new figure, Verethragna (Avestan: Vərəθraγna), "the Smiter of Resistance." His very name is the Avestan translation of Vṛtra-han, "Vṛtra-slayer," the epithet of Indra in the Rigveda. Verethragna appears in ten different forms: a strong wind, a bull, a white horse, a rutting camel, a boar, a youth, a raven, a wild ram, a fighting buck, and a man bearing a weapon of gold. Each form embodies a different aspect of victorious force. The Bahram Yasht devoted to him is among the most vivid of the Avestan hymns.

The Name Becomes a Royal Legacy

The Iranian word for "victory" (bahram or wahram) is derived from Verethragna's name. Numerous Sasanian kings bore names meaning "Victory": Bahram I, II, III, IV, V. The name spread into the wider Iranian cultural sphere: the Armenian Vahagn, the Parthian Varhran, and even a Sogdian borrowing into Central Asian culture. It is one of the great ironies of religious history: a god whose name means "I smash Vṛtra/Indra's dragon" became the greatest warrior-deity of the Iranian world, precisely by replacing the deity who had performed that function. Indra was cast out as a demon, but his dragon-slaying power was too essential to abandon. It simply found a new host.

The Paradox in Brief

Zoroastrianism condemned "Indra" as a fiend, yet worshipped Victory in another form whose name means "Indra's feat." On the flip side, later Hindu tradition also grew ambivalent about Indra, portraying him in epics and Purāṇas as proud, sometimes foolish, and subordinate to higher gods like Vishnu. In Buddhism, Indra (as Śakra) is a benevolent but still samsaric deity, not an ultimate savior. Thus both cultures, in different ways, moved beyond the old storm-god's primacy: Iran demonized him and preserved his function under a new name; India domesticated him within a larger pantheon.

The Chief Adversary: Angra Mainyu versus the Asura Kings

Perhaps the clearest case of conceptual divergence lies in each culture's choice of chief adversary. In Hindu myth, the adversaries of the Devas are often led by powerful Asura kings, including Vṛtra, Rāvaṇa, Hiraṇyakaśipu, and Mahishasura, who despite their might are ultimately on the side of adharma (chaos/unrighteousness). Zoroastrianism externalized the source of evil into a separate, nearly independent being, whereas Indian thought more often internalized it into cyclical struggles and personal karma. Despite these divergences, it is crucial to note that broadly similar values underlay both the Vedic and Zoroastrian systems. Both traditions honored truth, generosity, bravery, and loyalty, and abhorred breach of oaths, cruelty, and chaos. The Encyclopædia Iranica concludes that the demonization process in Iran was gradual and did not imply a wholly different morality: the daevas were rejected as unethical gods, not because Iranians worshipped cruelty while Indians worshipped kindness. In fact, "the good group and the bad group in both religions have broadly similar morality." The difference was in classification, not ethical substance.

It seems their ethics are not even so different as to worship each other's devils. On the surface at least, they have a lot in common. The quarrel was not about whether truth is good or falsehood is evil. It was about which beings embodied which qualities — and on that, the two traditions, through linguistic accident and cultural divergence, came to precisely opposite conclusions.

Chapter Seven

Dualism Entrenched

The Zoroastrian Revolution, Vedic Responses, and the Architecture of Good and Evil

By the middle of the first millennium BCE, the trajectories of the Iranian and Indian religions had crystallized into what we recognize today as Zoroastrianism on one hand, and the broad Vedic-derived Hindu tradition (and its offshoots like Buddhism) on the other. Zoroastrianism formalized perhaps the world's first fully fleshed-out dualist theology: a cosmic moral battlefield with clearly defined sides, each with its divine hierarchy and each with a role for every human soul to play.

While Ahura Mazda (called Ohrmazd in later Middle Persian) was the uncreated Creator of all that is good, Angra Mainyu (Ahriman) was the uncreated source of evil, not equal in power (for ultimately good will triumph at the eschaton), but terrifyingly potent in the current age. All the Ahuric beings (the Yazata gods and holy immortals like Mithra, Anāhitā, Sraosha, Haoma, and the six Amesha Spentas) rally under Mazda's banner, and all demonic forces (the Daevas and the druj-host) serve Ahriman. Life itself became a moral battleground. Every honest act was literally supporting the good gods against the evil ones, and every impure thought was a victory for Angra Mainyu.

At the end of time, after a series of saviors (Saoshyants), the dead will be resurrected, all creation will be purged of evil, and even hell will be emptied as souls undergo final purification through molten metal. Unlike the Hindu cyclical cosmos, which repeats endlessly through kalpas and yugas, the Zoroastrian cosmos has a beginning, a middle (our current age of struggle), and an end. At the end, a Saoshyant (savior) will appear, the dead will be resurrected, evil will be utterly destroyed, and creation will be renewed in a state of perfection. Hell will be emptied, even the demons rehabilitated through a final purgation of molten metal through which all souls pass. Remarkably, this means Zoroastrian eschatology is ultimately reconciliatory: even Angra Mainyu will be defeated and obliterated, and creation will be made whole.

Deep Dive: Zurvanite Heresy — The One Unity Behind the Two Principles

The Problem of Pure Dualism

Even within Zoroastrianism, the stark dualism of Ahura Mazda versus Angra Mainyu created a philosophical problem: if both are uncreated and self-existent, what guarantees that good will ultimately triumph over evil? Two eternal, uncreated principles locked in combat implies an unstable metaphysics. This tension gave rise to a significant theological movement known as Zurvanism.

Zurvanism: Time as the Parent of Both

In Zurvanite thought, which became significant in the Sasanian period (3rd-7th centuries CE), both Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu were conceived as twin sons of Zurvān (Infinite Time or Fate). The story went that Zurvān, a primordial being of unlimited time, performed sacrifices to obtain a son who would create the good world. After a thousand years of sacrifice he began to doubt, and from this doubt Angra Mainyu was born (doubt = evil impulse). At the same moment, Ahura Mazda was born from the pure sacrifice. Zurvān then granted dominion first to whichever son appeared first; Angra Mainyu burst forth and seized the promise, ruling for a limited period, after which Ohrmazd (Ahura Mazda) would take over eternally.

This narrative accomplishes something theologically radical: it places both good and evil under a higher principle (Zurvān/Time), implying that the universe has an underlying unity behind the duality. Orthodox Zoroastrians condemned this as heresy, precisely because it appeared to make Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu equal in origin and to undermine the absolute moral authority of Ahura Mazda. But Zurvanism was popular enough to influence the court theology of several Sasanian kings and to leave traces in later Islamic and Gnostic traditions that drew on Iranian sources.

The Parallel Indian Problem

India had the opposite problem: if all is ultimately Brahman (the single ultimate reality), then evil has no independent existence, yet clearly evil seems to exist. Hindu philosophers resolved this through the concept of māyā (cosmic illusion or the power of projection) and avidyā (ignorance). Evil is not ontologically real; it is a product of ignorance, a misperception of the One as many, of the eternal Self as the perishable body. Once you truly know Brahman, evil evaporates like a dream on waking. Zoroastrians resolved it apocalyptically: evil is real NOW but will be annihilated in the Final Renovation. Both approaches aimed at a final harmony, but one through absorption (Indian monism: duality dissolves into unity), the other through annihilation (Zoroastrian dualism: one side destroys the other).

Vedic religion, and the evolving Hindu tradition, responded to the challenge of dualism differently. Rather than a stark cosmic war with clearly defined sides, Indian thought absorbed the dichotomy into a more fluid and cyclical narrative. The war between Devas and Asuras became a perennial theme, not as an absolute beginning and end, but as a repeating motif in the fabric of the universe. The Puranas tell of Asuras who perform austerities and momentarily gain power, even rulership over the cosmos, only to be overthrown by the Devas aided by Vishnu or another supreme deity. However, critically, the Asuras in Hinduism were not the metaphysical equals of the supreme good. They were part of the samsaric cycle, often related by blood to the gods. Indeed "Asura" and "Deva" could be brothers: Prahlāda the Asura prince is the son of the demon king Hiraṇyakaśipu but devotee of Vishnu, illustrating perfectly blurred lines between the categories.

Hindu philosophy, especially by the time of the Upanishads and later the Bhagavad Gītā, internalized the deva-asura conflict as symbolic of human tendencies. The Gītā's 16th chapter enumerates daivi sampad (divine qualities: fearlessness, purity, compassion) versus asuri sampad (demonic qualities: pride, anger, ignorance) inherent in human nature. It explicitly says these traits exist mixed in people, and rarely is a person wholly one or the other. The implication is that gods and demons are in our hearts, not just in the heavens. This psychological interpretation shows the fundamentally different approach: while Zoroastrianism drew the battle lines in the cosmos, Hinduism drew them within the self.

This dualist creed had far-reaching influence beyond the borders of Iran. Many scholars have argued that Zoroastrian ideas, including one single good God opposed by a Devil, a final apocalypse, heaven and hell, and messianic saviors, profoundly influenced later Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. It is an alluring and well-evidenced thesis. When the Persians liberated the Jews from Babylonian captivity in 539 BCE, the Jews were extensively exposed to Zoroastrian doctrines, possibly inspiring concepts like Satan, angels, and resurrection in Second Temple Jewish thought. Zoroastrianism is one of the earliest religions to articulate ethics as the core of cosmic conflict, a revolutionary contribution to the world's moral imagination.

A Revolution in Human Moral Imagination

Earlier Indo-European religions (including the Vedic) had been more ritual-focused, concerned with maintaining cosmic order through sacrifice rather than moral dualism. Zoroaster flipped that script: elevating personal righteousness and the choice between good and evil to cosmic significance, essentially spiritualizing what had been largely a ritual affair. This was revolutionary for its time, and its influence radiates through Jewish angelology, Christian concepts of Satan, and Islamic ideas about Iblis. The deva-asura schism was not just an internal Indo-Iranian affair: it became one of the foundational conceptual structures of world religion.

Chapter Eight

The Abrahamic Crucible

How Zoroastrianism Shaped Judaism, Christianity, and Islam

No story of the Indo-Iranian schism is complete without addressing one of its most consequential echoes: the profound influence that Zoroastrian dualism, born from the deva-asura split, exerted upon the Abrahamic religions that would come to govern the moral imagination of half the world's population. The story begins in 597 BCE with an event that the Hebrew Bible records with a mixture of horror and theological reflection: the Babylonian exile.

When Nebuchadnezzar II of Babylon destroyed Jerusalem, burned the Temple of Solomon, and deported the Judean elite to Mesopotamia, he inadvertently created one of the most theologically generative encounters in history. In Babylon, the exiled Jews lived within sight of the great ziggurat temples of Marduk and Ishtar, absorbed Babylonian astronomical knowledge (which left permanent marks on Jewish ritual calendar), and heard stories of Persian religion filtering in from the east. The exposure to Zoroastrian concepts would accelerate dramatically a generation later.

Ahura Mazda and Yahweh were each understood as the one righteous God of all creation, the opponent of chaos and falsehood. The theological DNA was compatible.

The consequences for Jewish theology were immense. Before the Babylonian exile, the Hebrew Bible's picture of the divine world is relatively simple: Yahweh is supreme, other nations' gods are "foreign gods" (sometimes explicitly recognized as existing, sometimes denied), and evil enters the world through human disobedience. The Cyrus Cylinder, inscribed in Babylonian cuneiform, records the conquest of Babylon by Cyrus the Great and his policy of restoring deported peoples and their gods to their homelands. Often called the first charter of human rights, it represented in practice the Persian Zoroastrian policy of religious tolerance — a striking contrast to Xerxes' daiva inscription, which suppressed rival cults. The encounter of Zoroastrian Persia with exilic Judaism would leave indelible marks on Jewish, and through Judaism, Christian and Islamic theology.

After the exile and the sustained contact with Persian religion, the situation changed dramatically. In texts composed or compiled in the post-exilic period (6th-4th centuries BCE), including later prophetic books, the wisdom literature, and especially the apocalyptic texts, we see a transformation. The figure of "the satan" begins to acquire the characteristics of an independent malevolent principle, ultimately becoming Satan, the cosmic adversary of God and humanity. Angels multiply into elaborately organized hierarchies: seraphim, cherubim, archangels. Resurrection of the dead, virtually absent from pre-exilic Hebrew thought, becomes a prominent theological concept. A dramatic final judgment, in which all souls will be evaluated and assigned to eternal reward or punishment, emerges as a cornerstone of Second Temple Judaism. And an apocalyptic cosmic war between forces of good and evil, to be resolved at the end of time in God's ultimate victory, becomes the framework of entire religious texts.

These concepts align with remarkable precision to the Zoroastrian theological framework: the cosmic dualism of Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu, the elaborate angelic hierarchy of the Amesha Spentas and Yazatas, the resurrection of the dead at the Frashokereti, the final judgment at the Chinvat Bridge, and the eschatological triumph of good over evil. The correspondence is too systematic to be coincidental. The dominant scholarly interpretation is that Jewish theology absorbed these Zoroastrian structures during the Persian period and transformed them into distinctively Jewish forms, which then passed into Christianity and Islam.

Deep Dive: The Dead Sea Scrolls, the War Scroll, and the Sons of Light

The War Scroll (1QM)

Among the Dead Sea Scrolls found at Qumran in 1947, one of the most extraordinary is the War Scroll (1QM), composed probably in the 1st century BCE by a Jewish sectarian community that identified themselves as the "Sons of Light." The scroll describes in elaborate military detail the eschatological war between the Sons of Light (the community and their angelic allies) and the Sons of Darkness (the enemies of the community and their demonic allies), to be fought at the end of days under the leadership of the archangel Michael.

The language of the War Scroll is saturated with the Zoroastrian conceptual vocabulary of Light versus Darkness, Truth versus the Lie: "And you, O God, are terrifying in the glory of your kingdom, and the congregation of your holy ones is among us for everlasting help... [you will] deliver into the hand of the poor the enemies from all the lands, to humble the mighty of the peoples by the hand of those bent to the dust, to render to the wicked their reward, to justify for yourself the judgment of truth, and to make of your name an everlasting great one among the peoples..." — this is recognizably Asha-language, the language of Cosmic Truth triumphing over the Lie, applied to a specific Jewish sectarian context.

The Magi at the Nativity

The New Testament Gospel of Matthew (2:1-12) contains one of the most culturally evocative stories in Christian scripture: the Magi (Greek: magoi, from Old Persian maguš) come from the East to worship the infant Jesus, following a star. The Magi were specifically the priestly class of Zoroastrianism; the word "magus" (plural "magi") is the root of our English word "magic" and refers to the hereditary Median priesthood who maintained the sacred fire and performed the Zoroastrian rituals. The Matthew nativity narrative thus depicts Zoroastrian priests as the first non-Jewish worshippers of Christ, guided by celestial signs (a Zoroastrian concern with astronomical portents) to honor the one whom Matthew presents as the fulfillment of all prophecy.

Whether historical or theological legend, this story encapsulates the extraordinary theological encounter between the two traditions that flowed from the original Indo-Iranian heritage: the Zoroastrian priests, descendants of the tradition that demonized "Daevas" and elevated Ahura Mazda, appearing in the founding narrative of a religion that would claim universal dominion.

The influence of Zoroastrian dualism on Christianity and Islam is equally profound. Early Christianity's developing concept of Satan as an independent cosmic adversary of God, who rules the "world" as a usurping power until Christ's return, is structurally identical to Angra Mainyu's role in Zoroastrianism. The Christian concept of a final judgment, resurrection, heaven, and hell, the notion that history is moving toward an eschatological climax when good will finally triumph over evil, the existence of angels and demons as organized armies on opposing sides of a cosmic war: all of these have precise parallels in Zoroastrian theology that predate their appearance in Christian texts.

In Islam, the figure of Iblis, who refuses to bow before Adam and becomes Shaytan (Satan), the enemy of humanity, embodies a similar cosmic adversary role. Islamic cosmology's division of spiritual beings into the obedient angels (malak) and the rebellious jinn (among whom Iblis is supreme) echoes the Zoroastrian division of Yazatas (worthy of worship) and Daevas (rebellious evil spirits). The Islamic eschatology of the Day of Judgment, the weighing of deeds, the bridge over hell, and the final paradise all have clear structural parallels in Zoroastrian eschatology. This is the long shadow of the Indo-Iranian schism: the moment when Zarathustra declared that some divine beings were good and others evil was the moment that set in motion a chain of influence stretching from the Pontic steppe to the Quran's final chapter.

Chapter Nine

Echoes in Distant Lands

Comparative Reflections from Norse to Mesopotamia, Greek to Celtic

The Indo-Iranian divergence is a dramatic example of religious schism, but it is not without parallels in world mythology. It can be illuminating to zoom out and see how the themes of divine conflict, inversion, and reinterpretation appear in other Indo-European cultures and beyond. While no other case exactly mirrors the Vedic-Avestan reversal, we find analogous patterns everywhere: old gods supplanted by new, sibling rivalries among deities, and peoples projecting their rivals as demons in mythic terms.

Norse Mythology: Æsir, Vanir, and the War That Made Peace

In Norse mythology, we encounter the dichotomy of Æsir and Vanir, two tribes of gods who once warred but later reconciled. This is not a moral dualism (both Æsir and Vanir are regarded as good gods), but the very terminology carries the fingerprints of the Indo-European inheritance. The Æsir (singular áss, "god") stems from Proto-Germanic ansuz, which is cognate with the Proto-Indo-Iranian asura meaning a lord or spirit of vital power. Meanwhile, the generic word for gods in Norse, tívar (from deiwos), survived as a poetic term for the gods, and Týr, a specific sky-god, carries the same root as Sanskrit deva. The correspondence is striking: in Norse belief, the Æsir were the chief gods (Odin, Thor, etc.), and Týr, originally a sky father, had a background role by Viking times.

One could say the Norse Æsir equal Indo-Iranian Asuras/Ahuras (lords), and Norse tívar equal Devas/Daevas (gods in general). However, crucially, the Norse did not moralize this division as good versus evil. That distinction fell instead to the conflict between gods and giants (Jötunn). The giants embodied forces of chaos much like the Vedic Asuras turned Rakshasas or the Zoroastrian Daevas. Yet many Norse giants have ambiguous relationships with the gods: Loki is a giant's son adopted among gods; Skadi, a giantess, marries into the Æsir. This fluidity is oddly reminiscent of Indian myths where gods and demons share lineage and sometimes intermingle (Asuras were sons of Kashyapa just as Devas were, according to the Purāṇas).

Furthermore, Proto-Uralic asera, a word meaning "supreme spirit or lord" borrowed from Proto-Indo-Iranian, shows that long before the split, the concept of "Asura" as a great lord was prominent enough to be loaned into entirely unrelated language families. This suggests the term's extraordinary antiquity and prestige. The fact that Proto-Germanic ansuz (Aesir), Proto-Uralic asera, Sanskrit asura, and Avestan ahura all align indicates that "asura-lord" was one of the most fundamental conceptual exports of the Proto-Indo-Iranian cultural world.

Deep Dive: The Greek Titanomachy and the Universal War-of-Gods Pattern

The Pattern of Generational Succession

Across Indo-European mythologies, a remarkably consistent pattern emerges: the current order of gods came to power by displacing a prior order, and the displaced beings are associated with chaos, size, primordial power, and the raw forces of the world before human civilization. In Greek mythology, the Olympians (Zeus and his siblings) overthrow the Titans (Cronus and his generation), who had themselves overthrown the Ouranides (children of Uranus the Sky). In Norse myth, the Æsir (and Vanir) are in perpetual tension with the Jötunn (giants), the primordial beings. In Celtic Irish mythology, the divine Tuatha Dé Danann battle the Fomorians (dark, formless beings from the deep). In Vedic India, the Devas contend with the Asuras and Rakshasas. In Zoroastrian Iran, the Ahuras battle the Daevas.

What the Pattern Encodes

Georges Dumézil, the great French comparative mythologist of the 20th century, proposed that the Indo-European peoples shared a three-part social and ideological structure: a class of sacred sovereignty (priest-kings), a class of warriors, and a class of producers/farmers. Each class had its corresponding divine patrons. In the Indo-Iranian world, the sacred sovereignty class corresponded to Varuṇa/Ahura Mazda (cosmic order), the warrior class to Indra/Verethragna (martial victory), and the producers to the twin healers Nasatyas/Anāhitā (fertility and health). The "war of the gods" myths may reflect the tensions between these social functions, with the sovereignty and producer functions sometimes united against the purely martial function (explaining why warrior-gods like Indra can be ambivalent figures and why a reform like Zarathustra's could demonize the warrior-patron).

Coomaraswamy's Observation

Ananda Coomaraswamy, the great Sri Lankan-American scholar of Indian art and mythology, once observed that across Indo-European traditions, the relationship between gods and titans/asuras is one of potential identity: "the titan is potentially an angel, the angel still by nature a titan." In Hindu understanding, this means that the Asuras are not ontologically evil beings but beings who have let the lower qualities (rajas and tamas, passion and inertia) dominate their higher potential. They are, philosophically, what any being becomes when it forgets its divine nature and becomes consumed by ego and power. This is a psychologically sophisticated and compassionate view of evil: not a fixed essence but a directional failure, always potentially reversible.

Mesopotamia: Marduk, Tiamat, and the Combat Myth

The ancient Mesopotamian tradition offers another lens. The Babylonian creation epic Enuma ElishEnuma ElishThe Babylonian creation epic, composed c. 12th century BCE. It describes the primordial cosmos as chaos (Tiamat and Apsu, salt and fresh water), then the birth of the gods, Tiamat's war against them, and finally Marduk's defeat of Tiamat and creation of the world from her body. This "combat myth" has striking structural parallels to the Vedic story of Indra slaying the serpent Vṛtra and to the Norse Thor's battle with the Midgard Serpent Jörmungandr. Comparative mythologists identify a Proto-Indo-European "Dragon Slaying" myth: a hero-god defeats a chthonic water-serpent to liberate the cosmic waters and establish order. The myth is attested from India (Indra/Vṛtra) through Greece (Zeus/Typhon) to the Norse world (Thor/Jörmungandr) and likely reflects a common inheritance.

What makes the Mesopotamian case particularly relevant to the Indo-Iranian story is this: when the Iranians demonized Indra, they were in a sense doing what Marduk did to Tiamat on a theological rather than mythological plane. They were declaring the dragon-fighting principle itself (the warrior god Indra) to be on the wrong side of the cosmos, casting the dragon-slayer as a dragon. This is an inversion of the combat myth so complete it is almost dizzying. The Vedic Indra was the hero of the cosmic dragon fight; in Zoroastrian theology, Indra himself became the enemy of truth and order. The dragon-slaying power was preserved (in Verethragna), but the deity was displaced.

How Christianity Demonized the Old Gods

A pertinent parallel comes from the early Christianization of Europe. When Christianity spread into the Germanic, Celtic, and Slavic worlds, it encountered robust polytheistic traditions that resisted easy elimination. The strategy employed by many early missionaries was not unlike what happened in the Indo-Iranian schism: the old gods were declared to be demons. The Norse Odin, the Germanic Woden, the Slavic Perun and Veles, the Celtic horned god Cernunnos: all were recast in Christian missionary literature as devils or malevolent spirits deceiving the ignorant pagans.

This process of demonization left permanent traces in language and folklore. In Armenian Christianity, the word for pagan gods, dew (from Iranian daeva), came to mean "demon," showing direct influence of Zoroastrian dualism on the Armenian vocabulary after their conversion. And intriguingly, modern Persian language itself, though Islamic now, still uses div (from daeva) to mean a fiend or ogre in folklore, preserving that ancient legacy in everyday speech. The daeva has traveled from a Proto-Indo-European word for "shining one" to a Zoroastrian theological demon, to an Armenian Christian evil spirit, to a Persian Islamic ogre: the same root, across five thousand years, consistently pushed toward the demonic whenever a new theological order needed an adversary.

The Universal Pattern

We might say the "war of the gods" motif is Indo-European-wide: Greek mythology's Olympians versus Titans, the Norse Æsir versus Giants, Celtic tales of Tuatha Dé Danann versus Fomorians, Vedic Devas versus Asuras, Zoroastrian Ahuras versus Daevas: all reflect an archetypal story of an older order overcome by a newer, or an eternal rivalry between chaos and cosmos. The Indo-Iranian twist was to map that mythical clash onto their own cultural bifurcation: the Iranians in effect labeled the "former gods" (daevas) as wicked, while the Indians downplayed that narrative or reinterpreted it in endless cycles. Both were making sense of the same inherited template in the light of their own historical circumstances.

Part Five

The Modern Age Reacts

Colonial misreadings, nationalist reclamations, and the living dualities of our time

Chapter Ten

Knowledge Lost and Found

Colonial Encounters and Misreadings of the Ancient Past

As we leap forward into the modern age, the long chain of reinterpretation and misunderstanding continued in new forms. The Indo-Iranian religious divergence, after unfolding across millennia, eventually faded from active memory. Zoroastrianism dwindled after the Islamic conquests of Iran in the 7th century CE, surviving in pockets (notably among the Parsi community who fled to India, and among the Irani Zoroastrians who remained in Iran). Hinduism and Buddhism evolved in their own directions. When European scholars "rediscovered" these ancient connections in the 18th and 19th centuries, it was like awakening sleeping giants, but the process was rife with its own biases and misreadings.

In the 19th century, the field of comparative philology blossomed. The realization that Sanskrit, Avestan, Greek, Latin, and other languages were related (members of the Indo-European family) was revolutionary. Scholars like Max Müller pointed out that Sanskrit deva and Avestan daeva were the same word, split by a quirk of religious history. Early theories tried to explain this inversion as the result of a massive, ancient religious war between the Indians and Iranians. Modern scholarship, however, suggests a much slower, more complex evolution of language and localized theology, shaped by geography, politics, and the profound reforms of figures like Zarathustra.

In the 19th and 20th centuries, these ancient texts were sometimes twisted for modern political ends. The concept of the "Aryan" (from Arya/Airya), originally a noble cultural self-designation for both Indo-Aryans and Iranians, was tragically co-opted by European racial theorists. They stripped the term of its spiritual and linguistic context, fashioning it into a weapon of supremacy that fueled some of the darkest chapters of modern history.

Yet, underneath the scars of modern misinterpretation and the millennia of theological division, the original texts still speak. They reveal a shared human heritage grappling with the same fundamental questions of order, truth, and the nature of the divine. The story of the Devas and Asuras reminds us that our most profound religious divisions often stem from shared roots, and that our "demons" might simply be our neighbors' "gods" seen through a mirror darkly.

Chronology of the Schism

c. 3500 – 2500 BCE
Proto-Indo-European Unity
Pastoralists on the Pontic-Caspian steppe share a common pantheon, including the Sky Father (Dyēus Ph₂tēr) and early concepts of cosmic order. The terms *deiwos (god) and *h₂nsu- (lord) are established.
c. 2100 – 1700 BCE
Sintashta & BMAC Interaction
Proto-Indo-Iranians develop chariot warfare and sophisticated fire rituals. In the BMAC zone, they likely refine the Soma/Haoma ceremonies before splitting into distinct Indic and Iranian migrations.
c. 1500 – 1200 BCE
Composition of the Rigveda
In the northwestern Indian subcontinent, Indo-Aryans compose the earliest Vedic hymns. "Asura" is still frequently used as a title of immense honor for high gods like Varuṇa and Mitra.
c. 1200 – 1000 BCE
Zarathustra's Reform
On the Iranian plateau, the prophet Zarathustra composes the Gathas. He elevates Ahura Mazda to supreme status and establishes a strict ethical dualism, laying the groundwork for the demonization of the Daevas.
c. 800 – 500 BCE
The Semantic Inversion Solidifies
In India, later Vedic texts and early epics increasingly cast Asuras as the enemies of the Devas. In Iran, the Vendidad explicitly lists Vedic gods like Indra and the Nasatyas as chief demons.
c. 539 BCE
The Abrahamic Encounter
Cyrus the Great conquers Babylon, freeing the exiled Jews. The subsequent exposure to Persian Zoroastrianism deeply influences Jewish (and later Christian and Islamic) concepts of angels, demons, dualism, and the apocalypse.
c. 486 – 465 BCE
Xerxes' Daiva Inscription
Achaemenid King Xerxes I erects an inscription at Persepolis boasting of destroying a sanctuary of the "daivas," officially cementing the theological schism as imperial policy.
